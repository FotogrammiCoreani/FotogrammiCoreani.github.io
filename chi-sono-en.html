Who’s behind the blog

An Italian guy
in love with cinema
who still believes it has something to say

Fotogrammi Coreani was born from a simple belief: that kdrama is not niche entertainment, but one of the few places left where cinema still speaks honestly about us.

A letter to those reading

There is a kind of attention that Western cinema has forgotten how to practice. If you are here, you probably already know what I mean. That feeling certain Korean dramas leave behind: something moving beneath the surface that refuses to disappear once the episode ends. A silence. A line of dialogue. The way two characters look at each other without saying anything. And suddenly you realize the cinema you used to watch no longer feels enough in quite the same way.

My name is Leonardo, and I started this blog because I needed a place where kdrama could be discussed the way it deserves — not through simple references and plot summaries, but with the same attention reserved for cinema that truly matters. Because kdrama, at least the kind I care about, is cinema that matters.

I believe Western cinema is exhausting its narrative substance. Korean cinema, on the other hand, still has something to say.

I have a conviction — perhaps a questionable one, but I care deeply about it. Some would argue that real Korea is far removed from the polished narratives of dramas: the slow romances, the quiet landscapes, the feeling that life moves at a different rhythm. And they would be right. The point is that Korean cinema does not portray Korea as it is. It portrays the Korea it wishes to become. And it is precisely within that distance between reality and desire that the most compelling Korean cinema emerges. Its best writers and directors work almost like antibodies: they identify the distortions of a society that often recognizes itself as deeply dystopian, and transform those distortions into stories.

In the West, we are still far from this kind of awareness. Perhaps that is why certain dramas hurt so much — and heal so much at the same time.

Then there is the question of love. In kdrama, love is not an event — not the kiss, not the confession, not the narrative turning point. It is a process built through attention: small gestures, shared silences, the memory of a detail the other person thought insignificant. A vision that Western cinema has almost entirely abandoned, perhaps because it requires time — and time has become the luxury nobody can afford anymore.

Fotogrammi Coreani is where I recover that sense of time. Review after review, episode after episode. Slowly — much like the dramas I love.
